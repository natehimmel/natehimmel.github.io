About Me

I am a biologist studying how organisms sense their external environment and the origins and evolution of sensory systems, with particular expertise in ion channels, pain, thermosensation, and chemosensation.

I presently work as a Human Frontier Science Program postdoctoral fellow in the lab of Dr. Richard Benton, at the University of Lausanne. I'm generally interested in the evolution of the molecular components sensory systems, how the underlying genes and proteins may have evolved and functioned in early life (millions to billions of years ago), and how this may have contributed to the emergence and diversification of sensory nervous systems in animals.

My current projects seek to understand the function of sensory receptors of unknown function, and to resolve the evolutionary history of receptors critical to the function of sensory nervous systems.

Latest Publication

Insect Odorant receptors (Ors) and Gustatory receptors (Grs) are exceptionally diverse groups of receptors with a cloudy evolutionary history. Building upon previous work identifying non-animal homologs of these receptors, Richard Benton & I describe a unified family of eukaryotic "Seven Transmembrane Ion Channels" (7TMICs), with representatives across Eukaryota (including humans), dating back to at least the Last Eukaryotic Common Ancestor (otherwise known as LECA).

Benton R* and Himmel NJ*. Structural screens identify candidate human homologs of insect chemoreceptors and cryptic Drosophila gustatory receptor-like proteins. Preprint in bioRxiv, 2022.
