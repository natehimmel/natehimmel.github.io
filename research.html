Research Interests | Nociception, Pain, and Evolution

Nociception

How insects sense and respond to noxious stimuli

My graduate work in the Cox lab concerns how insects sense and respond to a variety of noxious stimuli, in particular noxious cold.

Some is known concerning how insects survive cold bouts, but less is known about how they might sense and respond to noxious low temperatures. In the lab, we are attempting to rectify this using various genetic, molecular, optical, and behavioral techniques.

Our work has largely focused on Transient Receptor Potential (TRP) channels — e.g., nompC, Trpm, and Pkd2 — and other related factors.

Evolution

How did sensory systems evolve?

A growing body of evidence points toward ancient origins for how our sensory systems function, including how we detect noxious, potentially painful stimuli.

I am broadly interested in how the molecular aspects of sensory systems evolved. Most recently, we phylogenetically characterized TRPM and TRPA channels among bilaterians, further evidencing that a variety of TRPM and TRPA functions predate the protostome (e.g, Drosophila) deuterostome (e.g., humans) split, some 550 million years ago.

  • Learn More in Himmel et al., 2019

Ion & Water Homeostasis

Function and disfunction in epithelial transport

My post-baccalaureate work with Mitsi Blount sought to understand how epithelial transport, nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (NDI; a common side-effect of lithium treament), and Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD; inherited mutations in polycystin TRP genes) function.

Work in the Blount lab has identified new disease-associated epithelial cells, potential targets for treating NDI and PKD, as well as elucidated basic mechanisms of epithelial transport.
