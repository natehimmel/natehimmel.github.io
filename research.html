Research Interests | Sensory Systems, Pain, Ion Channels, and Evolution

Sensory Systems

How do organisms sense and respond to their environment?

My graduate work in the Cox lab concerned how insects sense and respond to a variety of noxious stimuli, in particular noxious cold.

Some is known concerning how insects survive cold bouts, but less is known about how they might sense and respond to noxious low temperatures. In the lab, and with our collaborators, we are attempting to rectify this using various genetic, molecular, optical, and behavioral techniques. Our work has largely focused on Transient Receptor Potential (TRP) channels — e.g., nompC, Trpm, and Pkd2 — and other related factors.

  • Anoctamins and chloride physiology in pain (Himmel et al, 2021)
  • A sensory neural basis for Drosophila cold acclimation (Himmel et al, 2021)
  • Drosophila menthol nociception (Himmel et al., 2019)
  • Drosophila acid nociception (Lopez-Bellido et al., 2019)
  • Review, invertebrate nociception (Himmel*, Patel*, and Cox, 2017)
  • Drosophila cold nociception (Turner* and Armengol* et al., 2016)

Evolution

How did sensory systems evolve?

A growing body of evidence points toward ancient origins for how our sensory systems function, including how we detect noxious, potentially painful stimuli.

I am broadly interested in how the molecular aspects of sensory systems evolved. My graduate work has unconvered evidence that the functions of TRPM and TRPA channels may predate the protostome (e.g, Drosophila) deuterostome (e.g., humans) split, some 550 million years ago. Our work has also led to the discovery a new family of TRP channel, which we call TRP soromelastatin (TRPS), as well as reorganized the TRPM family, challenging a long-standing, vertebrate-centric view of the clade.

  • Review, TRP channel phylogeny (Himmel & Cox, 2020)
  • TRPS and the reorganization of TRPM (Himmel et al., 2020)
  • On menthol and ancient TRP channels (Himmel et al., 2019)

Ion Channels and Homeostasis

Function and disfunction in ion transport

My post-baccalaureate work with Mitsi Blount sought to understand how epithelial transport, nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (NDI; a common side-effect of lithium treament), and Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD; inherited mutations in polycystin TRP genes) function.

Work in the Blount lab has identified new disease-associated epithelial cells, potential targets for treating NDI and PKD, as well as elucidated basic mechanisms of epithelial transport.

This work has continued in my graduate work, where I studied TRP channels, and chloride ion physiology functions in sensory neurons and neuropathic pain.

  • Anoctamins and chloride physiology in pain (Himmel et al, 2021)
  • Review, TRP channel phylogeny (Himmel & Cox, 2020)
  • TRPS and the reorganization of TRPM (Himmel et al., 2020)
  • Purinergic signaling in UT-A1/A3 KO (Himmel et al., 2021)
  • Lithium and Pendrin relocalization (Himmel et al., 2018)
  • PKC-Alpha and nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (Sim et al., 2014)
