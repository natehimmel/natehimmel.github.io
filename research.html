Research Interests | Sensory Systems, Ion Channels, and Evolution

Sensory Systems

How do organisms sense and respond to their environment?

Our ability to sense the outside world is undeniably important -- but how do we, and other forms of life, do it?

My postdoctoral work in the Benton lab seeks to understand how receptors of unknown function work in a wide selection of taxa.

My graduate work in the Cox lab concerned how insects sense and respond to cold and chemicals, and how this was relevant to their ecology. This work also sought to understand how sensory neurons could differentiate between different types of sensory modalities (e.g. temperature and touch), and related mechanisms underlying human neuropathic pain.

  • Anoctamins and chloride physiology in pain (Himmel et al, 2023)
  • The neural basis of cold acclimation in flies (Himmel et al, 2021)
  • Drosophila menthol nociception (Himmel et al., 2019)
  • Review, invertebrate nociception (Himmel*, Patel*, and Cox, 2017)

Evolution

How have sensory systems evolved?

I am broadly interested in how the molecular components of sensory systems evolved -- when and how they emerged, and how they have diversified over the course of millions to billions of years.

In my current work in the Benton group, I am using cutting-edge methods in bioinformatics and phylogenetics to discover, categorize, and characterize cryptic receptor protein families.

My previous graduate work focused on the evolution of TRP channels, largely on their diversification in animals.

  • Evolutionary analysis of the 7TMIC superfamily (Himmel et al., 2023)
  • Human homologs of insect chemosensors (Benton & Himmel, 2023)
  • Review, TRP channel phylogeny (Himmel & Cox, 2020)
  • TRPS and the reorganization of TRPM (Himmel et al., 2020)

Ion Channels and Homeostasis

Function and disfunction in ion transport

Ion channels are fundamental components of sensory transduction, among other processes.

My post-baccalaureate work with Mitsi Blount sought to understand how epithelial transport, nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (NDI; a common side-effect of lithium treament), and Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD; inherited mutations in polycystin TRP genes) function. Work in the Blount lab has identified new disease-associated epithelial cells, potential targets for treating NDI and PKD, as well as elucidated basic mechanisms of epithelial transport.

This continued in my graduate work, where I studied TRP channels and ion homeostasis in the context of thermosensation and neuropathic pain. It also continues in my postdoctoral work, which focuses on cryptic ion channels of unknown function.

  • Purinergic signaling in UT-A1/A3 KO (Himmel et al., 2021)
  • Lithium and Pendrin relocalization (Himmel et al., 2018)
  • PKC-Alpha and nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (Sim et al., 2014)
